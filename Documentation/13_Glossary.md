## A

**Accelerator**
A fixed-term program (typically 3-6 months) that provides funding, mentorship, and resources to startups in exchange for equity (typically 5-10%). Examples: Y Combinator, Techstars, 500 Startups.

**Accredited Investor**
An individual or entity that meets certain income ($200K+ annually) or net worth ($1M+ excluding primary residence) requirements, allowing them to invest in unregistered securities like startup equity.

**Acquisition**
When one company purchases another company. Common exit strategy for startups.

**Agile**
An iterative approach to software development and project management that emphasizes flexibility, collaboration, and rapid delivery in short cycles (sprints).

**Angel Investor**
A high-net-worth individual who invests personal funds into early-stage startups, typically $25K-$500K. Often provides mentorship in addition to capital.

**Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR)**
The value of contracted recurring revenue normalized to a one-year period. ARR = MRR Ã— 12. Key metric for SaaS businesses.

**Anti-Dilution Provision**
A clause in preferred stock that protects investors from dilution if the company raises money at a lower valuation in the future ("down round").

**API (Application Programming Interface)**
A set of rules and protocols that allows different software applications to communicate with each other. Essential for integrations and third-party developers.

**Articles of Incorporation**
The legal document filed with the state to form a corporation. Includes company name, purpose, stock structure, and registered agent.

**At-Will Employment**
Employment relationship where either the employer or employee can terminate the relationship at any time, for any legal reason, with or without notice.

**Attorney-Work Product**
Documents prepared by lawyers in anticipation of litigation, which are generally protected from discovery and disclosure.

## B

**B2B (Business-to-Business)**
A business model where a company sells products or services to other businesses. Example: Salesforce, Slack.

**B2C (Business-to-Consumer)**
A business model where a company sells directly to individual consumers. Example: Netflix, Amazon.

**B2B2C (Business-to-Business-to-Consumer)**
A model where a company sells to businesses who then sell to consumers. Example: Payment processors, white-label solutions.

**Backend**
The server-side of an application that users don't see. Includes databases, servers, and APIs that power the frontend.

**Balance Sheet**
A financial statement showing a company's assets, liabilities, and shareholder equity at a specific point in time.

**Benchmark**
A standard or point of reference against which performance can be measured. In startups, often refers to industry-standard metrics.

**Beta**
A pre-release version of a product tested by a limited group of users to gather feedback and identify bugs before the official launch.

**Bootstrapping**
Building and growing a company using personal funds, revenue, or minimal outside investment, without venture capital.

**Board of Directors**
A group of individuals elected to represent shareholders and oversee company management. Typically includes founders, investors, and independent members.

**Boilerplate**
Standard language used in legal documents, contracts, or code that can be reused with minimal changes.

**Branding**
The process of creating a unique name, design, and image for a product or company in consumers' minds.

**Break-Even Point**
The point at which total revenue equals total costs, meaning the business is not making or losing money.

**Bridge Loan/Round**
Short-term financing to "bridge" a company to its next major financing round or milestone. Often done via convertible note.

**Burn Rate**
The rate at which a company spends its cash reserves, typically measured monthly. Critical for calculating runway.

**Business Model**
How a company creates, delivers, and captures value. Describes how a business makes money.

**Business Model Canvas**
A one-page strategic tool that maps out nine key business components on a single sheet. Alternative to a full business plan.

**Buyout**
The purchase of a controlling interest in a company, often used to refer to the complete acquisition of a company.

**Bylaws (Corporate Bylaws)**
Internal rules governing how a corporation operates, including board meetings, voting procedures, and officer duties.

## C

**C-Corporation (C-Corp)**
A legal structure for corporations where the business is taxed separately from its owners. Standard for VC-backed startups. Allows unlimited shareholders.

**CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)**
The total cost of acquiring a new customer, including marketing and sales expenses. Formula: (Marketing + Sales Costs) / New Customers.

**Cap Table (Capitalization Table)**
A spreadsheet showing ownership stakes, equity dilution, and value of equity in a company. Tracks who owns what percentage.

**Capital**
Financial assets or resources that a company uses to fund its operations and growth.

**Capital Raise**
The process of raising money from investors for your business.

**Cash Flow**
The movement of money in and out of a business. Positive cash flow means more money coming in than going out.

**Cash Flow Statement**
A financial statement that shows how cash enters and leaves a business over a specific period.

**CEO (Chief Executive Officer)**
The highest-ranking executive in a company, responsible for overall strategy and operations.

**CTO (Chief Technology Officer)**
The executive responsible for technology strategy and technical execution, typically the senior-most technical person.

**Churn Rate**
The percentage of customers who stop using your product over a given time period. Formula: (Lost Customers / Total Customers) Ã— 100.

**Cliff (Vesting Cliff)**
A period (typically one year) before any equity vests. If you leave before the cliff, you get 0%. After the cliff, you get a lump sum (usually 25%).

**Cohort Analysis**
Analyzing the behavior of groups of users who share a common characteristic (e.g., signed up in the same month).

**Common Stock**
Basic ownership shares in a company, typically held by founders and employees. Ranked below preferred stock in liquidation.

**Competitive Analysis**
Research on competitors to understand their strengths, weaknesses, strategies, and market position.

**Compliance**
Adhering to laws, regulations, standards, and industry guidelines relevant to your business.

**ConvertibleNote**
A form of short-term debt that converts into equity during a future financing round, typically with a discount or valuation cap.

**Convertible**
Short for convertible note or convertible security - debt that converts to equity.

**Conversion Rate**
The percentage of users who complete a desired action (e.g., free to paid, visitor to signup). Formula: (Conversions / Total Visitors) Ã— 100.

**Co-Founder**
One of the original founders of a startup, typically sharing significant equity and responsibility.

**Copyright**
Legal right that grants the creator of original work exclusive rights to its use and distribution.

**Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)**
Direct costs attributable to producing goods sold by a company. For SaaS, often includes hosting and support costs.

**CPA (Certified Public Accountant)**
A licensed accountant who can perform audits, prepare tax returns, and provide financial advice.

**CRM (Customer Relationship Management)**
Software for managing interactions with customers and potential customers. Examples: Salesforce, HubSpot.

**Crowdfunding**
Raising small amounts of money from a large number of people, typically via online platforms like Kickstarter or Indiegogo.

**Customer Persona**
A semi-fictional representation of your ideal customer based on market research and real data.

**Customer Success**
A business methodology focused on ensuring customers achieve their desired outcomes while using your product, thereby reducing churn.

## D

**DAU (Daily Active Users)**
The number of unique users who engage with your product on a given day.

**Data Room**
A secure online repository of company information shared with investors during due diligence.

**DBA (Doing Business As)**
A registered name under which a business operates that's different from its legal name. Also called a "trade name."

**Debt Financing**
Raising money by borrowing, which must be repaid with interest. Opposite of equity financing.

**Deck**
Short for pitch deck - a presentation for investors showcasing your business.

**Depreciation**
The gradual decrease in value of an asset over time, used for accounting and tax purposes.

**Dilution**
The reduction in ownership percentage that occurs when new shares are issued. Happens during funding rounds.

**DIN (Director Identification Number)**
A unique identification number required for company directors in some countries (e.g., India).

**Disruptive Innovation**
An innovation that creates a new market and value network, eventually disrupting existing markets.

**Distribution**
How you deliver your product or service to customers. Also refers to profit distributions to shareholders.

**Down Round**
A financing round where the company raises money at a lower valuation than the previous round. Generally unfavorable.

**Drag-Along Rights**
Rights enabling majority shareholders to force minority shareholders to join in the sale of a company.

**Due Diligence**
The investigation and analysis a prudent investor undertakes before investing. Involves reviewing financials, legal documents, and business operations.

---

## E

**Early Stage**
The phase of company development that includes seed and Series A funding rounds, typically when the company is establishing product-market fit.

**EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization)**
A measure of a company's operational profitability. Often used to value companies.

**Ecosystem**
The network of businesses, partners, customers, and other stakeholders that interact with your company.

**EdTech**
Technology companies focused on education and learning.

**EIN (Employer Identification Number)**
A unique nine-digit number assigned by the IRS to businesses for tax purposes. Required for payroll and banking.

**Elevator Pitch**
A brief (30-60 second) persuasive speech about your business, short enough to deliver during an elevator ride.

**Employee Stock Option Pool**
Shares set aside for future employee equity compensation, usually 10-20% of total shares.

**Equity**
Ownership interest in a company, typically represented by shares of stock.

**Equity Financing**
Raising money by selling ownership stakes (shares) in the company. Opposite of debt financing.

**ESOP (Employee Stock Ownership Plan)**
A program that provides employees with an ownership interest in the company through stock options.

**Exit**
How founders and investors cash out their equity. Common exits: acquisition, IPO, or secondary sale.

**Exit Strategy**
The plan for how founders and investors will realize returns on their investment, typically through acquisition or IPO.

---

## F

**Financial Projections**
Forecasts of future financial performance, typically including revenue, expenses, and cash flow for 3-5 years.

**FinTech**
Technology companies focused on financial services and products.

**First Mover Advantage**
The competitive advantage gained by being the first company to enter a market or launch a product category.

**Founding Team**
The initial group of individuals who start and build a company.

**Freemium**
A business model offering basic features for free while charging for advanced features. Examples: Spotify, Dropbox.

**Frontend**
The client-side of an application that users interact with directly. Includes the user interface and user experience.

**Fully Diluted**
The total number of shares if all possible sources of conversion (options, convertibles, etc.) are exercised.

**Funding Round**
A discrete instance of raising capital from investors. Typically named in series: Seed, Series A, B, C, etc.

---

## G

**GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles)**
Standard accounting rules and guidelines used in the United States.

**Go-to-Market Strategy (GTM)**
The plan for how you'll launch and sell your product to customers, including target market, distribution channels, and marketing approach.

**Grant (Stock Grant)**
Shares given to an employee or founder, often subject to vesting.

**Gross Margin**
Revenue minus cost of goods sold, expressed as a percentage of revenue. Formula: (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue Ã— 100.

**Gross Merchandise Value (GMV)**
The total sales value of merchandise sold through a marketplace over a specific time period.

**Growth Hacking**
Marketing techniques focused on rapid experimentation across channels to identify the most effective ways to grow a business.

**Growth Stage**
The phase after a company has achieved product-market fit and is focusing on rapid scaling.

---

## H

**Hardware Startup**
A company that creates physical products rather than software.

**Hockey Stick Growth**
Rapid exponential growth that creates a chart resembling a hockey stick lying flat.

**Holding Company**
A parent corporation that owns enough voting stock in another company to control its policies and management.

---

## I

**Incubator**
An organization that provides resources, mentorship, and sometimes office space to early-stage startups, typically without taking equity or with minimal equity.

**Indie Hacker**
Entrepreneur who builds and runs a business independently, often bootstrapped, focused on profitability over growth.

**Initial Public Offering (IPO)**
The first time a company sells shares to the public on a stock exchange. A type of exit.

**Intellectual Property (IP)**
Creations of the mind (inventions, designs, brand names) that can be owned and legally protected through patents, trademarks, and copyrights.

**IP Assignment Agreement**
Legal document where employees/contractors assign ownership of their inventions and creations to the company.

**Iteration**
A cycle of building, testing, and refining a product based on feedback.

---

## J

**J-Curve**
The shape of returns in venture capital, where early losses are followed by later gains.

**Jurisdiction**
The geographical area or legal authority under which a business operates and is governed.

**Just-in-Time (JIT)**
A strategy to increase efficiency and decrease waste by receiving goods only as they're needed.

---

## K

**Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)**
Measurable values that demonstrate how effectively a company is achieving key business objectives.

**Kickstarter**
A popular rewards-based crowdfunding platform for creative projects.

---

## L

**Landing Page**
A standalone web page created specifically for a marketing or advertising campaign where visitors "land" after clicking a link.

**Late Stage**
Companies that have achieved significant scale and are preparing for an exit (IPO or acquisition). Typically Series C and beyond.

**Launch**
The introduction of a new product or company to the market.

**Lead Investor**
The primary investor in a funding round who typically negotiates terms and contributes the largest amount.

**Lean Canvas**
A one-page business plan template adapted from the Business Model Canvas, designed specifically for startups.

**Lean Startup**
A methodology for developing businesses and products based on building an MVP, measuring results,and learning from customer feedback.

**Letter of Intent (LOI)**
A non-binding agreement outlining the preliminary understanding between parties before a detailed agreement is reached.

**Leverage**
Using borrowed capital or resources to increase the potential return on investment.

**Limited Liability Company (LLC)**
A business structure that combines the pass-through taxation of a partnership with the limited liability of a corporation.

**Liquidation Preference**
The right of investors to get their money back before other shareholders in the event of a sale or liquidation. Typically "1x" means they get their investment back first.

**Liquidity Event**
An event that allows shareholders to cash out, such as an acquisition or IPO.

**LTV (Lifetime Value)**
The total revenue a business can expect from a single customer over the relationship. Formula: (Average Revenue per Customer) Ã— (Gross Margin) / (Churn Rate).

---

## M

**MAU (Monthly Active Users)**
The number of unique users who engage with your product in a given month.

**Marketplace**
A platform that connects buyers and sellers, facilitating transactions. Examples: Airbnb, Etsy, Uber.

**Minimum Viable Product (MVP)**
The simplest version of a product that delivers core value and allows you to test key assumptions with real users.

**MoM (Month-over-Month)**
A comparison of performance between consecutive months, typically expressed as a percentage.

**Monetization**
The method(s) by which a business generates revenue from its product or service.

**Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR)**
The amount of predictable revenue generated each month from subscriptions.

**MoU (Memorandum of Understanding)**
A non-binding agreement between parties outlining mutual understanding and intentions.

**MVP (Minimum Viable Product)**
The version of a product with just enough features to gather validated learning about customers.

---

## N

**NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement)**
A legal contract that prevents parties from sharing confidential information with others.

**Net Income**
Total revenue minus total expenses. Also called "bottom line" or net profit.

**Net Promoter Score (NPS)**
A metric measuring customer satisfaction and loyalty. Customers rate on 0-10 scale how likely they are to recommend your product.

**Network Effect**
When a product becomes more valuable as more people use it. Classic example: Facebook, telephone networks.

**Non-Compete Agreement**
A clause preventing an employee from working for competitors or starting a competing business for a specified period after leaving.

**Non-Dilutive Funding**
Money raised that doesn't require giving up equity. Examples: grants, revenue-based financing, debt.

---

## O

**Onboarding**
The process of helping new users or employees get started with your product or company.

**Operating Agreement**
The document outlining governance, ownership, and operating procedures for an LLC.

**Operating Expenses (OpEx)**
Day-to-day costs of running a business, excluding COGS.

**Option Pool**
Shares reserved for future employees as part of equity compensation. See ESOP.

**Organic Growth**
Growth achieved through internal resources and optimization rather than paid advertising or acquisitions.

**Outsourcing**
Contracting work to external companies or freelancers rather than hiring full-time employees.

---

## P

**P&L (Profit and Loss Statement)**
Financial statement showing revenues, costs, and expenses over a period. Also called Income Statement.

**Patent**
Legal protection for an invention, giving the creator exclusive rights to make, use, and sell it for a period of time (typically 20 years).

**Payroll**
The process of paying employees, including calculating wages, withholding taxes, and distributing paychecks.

**Pitch**
A persuasive presentation of your business to investors, customers, or other stakeholders.

**Pitch Deck**
A slide presentation (typically 10-15 slides) used to pitch your business to investors.

**Pivot**
A strategic shift in business model, product, or target market based on learnings and market feedback.

**Platform**
A business model that creates value by facilitating exchanges between two or more interdependent groups, usually consumers and producers.

**Post-Money Valuation**
The value of a company immediately after receiving investment. Formula: Pre-money + Investment.

**Pre-Money Valuation**
The value of a company before receiving new investment.

**Preferred Stock**
A class of ownership with special rights, typically held by investors. Has priority over common stock in liquidation.

**Pre-Seed**
The earliest stage of fundingbefore a company has formalized or has a product, typically from friends, family, or angels.

**Private Company**
A company whose shares are not traded on public stock exchanges.

**Product-Market Fit**
When a product successfully satisfies strong market demand. Signs: organic growth, high retention, customers actively seeking you out.

**Product Requirements Document (PRD)**
A document outlining what a product should do, for whom, and why. Guides development.

**Profit Margin**
The percentage of revenue that becomes profit. Formula: (Net Income / Revenue) Ã— 100.

**Pro Rata Rights**
The right of existing investors to invest in future rounds to maintain their ownership percentage.

**Prototype**
An early sample or model of a product built to test a concept or process.

**PTO (Paid Time Off)**
Employee vacation days, sick leave, and personal days.

**Public Company**
A company whose shares are traded on public stock exchanges and must comply with SEC regulations.

---

## Q

**Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS)**
Stock in a C-Corp that may qualify for significant tax exemptions (up to $10M or 10x returns) under IRS Section 1202.

**Quarterly Business Review (QBR)**
A meeting held every three months to review performance, metrics, and strategy.

**Queue**
A list of tasks, features, or items waiting to be processed or completed. In development: backlog.

**Quick Ratio (SaaS)**
Measure of growth efficiency. Formula: (New MRR + Expansion MRR) / (Churned MRR + Contraction MRR).

---

## R

**Ramen Profitable**
Making just enough profit to cover founders' basic living expenses.

**Recurring Revenue**
Revenue that is predictable and expected to continue. Key for SaaS businesses.

**Referral**
When existing customers recommend your product to others, often incentivized through referral programs.

**Remote Work**
Working from a location outside the traditional office, often from home.

**Retention Rate**
The percentage of customers who continue using your product over time. Formula: ((Customers at End - New Customers) / Customers at Start) Ã— 100.

**Revenue**
The total income generated by the sale of goods or services.

**Revenue Model**
How a company generates income. Examples: subscriptions, advertising, transaction fees.

**Roadmap**
A strategic plan outlining the future direction and development of a product over time.

**ROI (Return on Investment)**
The ratio of net profit to investment. Formula: (Gain from Investment - Cost of Investment) / Cost of Investment Ã— 100.

**Round**
See Funding Round.

**Royalty**
Payment to a creator or owner for the use of their property or intellectual property, usually a percentage of revenue.

**Runway**
The amount of time a company can operate before running out of money. Formula: Cash Balance / Monthly Burn Rate.

---

## S

**SaaS (Software as a Service)**
Software delivered over the internet on a subscription basis rather than installed locally. Examples: Salesforce, Gmail.

**SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)**
An investment instrument created by Y Combinator that converts to equity in a future priced round. Simpler than convertible notes.

**Sales Funnel**
The journey potential customers go through from awareness to purchase, visualized as a funnel narrowing at each stage.

**SAM (Serviceable Available Market)**
The portion of the total addressable market that you can realistically serve with your product and business model.

**Scalability**
The capacity of a business to grow without being constrained by its structure or resources.

**Scrum**
An Agile framework for managing product development through iterative sprints, daily standups, and retrospectives.

**Secondary Sale**
When existing shareholders sell their shares to other investors, rather than the company issuing new shares.

**Seed Round**
The first significant round of funding (typically $250K-$2M) to develop the product and achieve initial traction.

**SEO (Search Engine Optimization)**
The practice of optimizing websites to rank higher in search engine results.

**Series A, B, C**
Sequential rounds of funding after the seed round, typically larger amounts at increasing valuations.

**Shareholder**
An individual or entity that owns shares in a company.

**Skateholder Agreement**
Legal contract defining rights and obligations of shareholders.

**Shares**
Units of ownership in a company. Also called stock or equity.

**SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market)**
The portion of SAM that you can realistically capture, typically in the first few years.

**Sprint**
A fixed time period (typically 1-2 weeks) during which a development team works to complete a set amount of work.

**Stakeholder**
Anyone with an interest in your business, including founders, investors, employees, customers, and partners.

**Startup**
A temporary organization designed to search for a repeatable and scalable business model.

**Stock Options**
The right to purchase shares at a predetermined price (strike price) in the future, typically granted to employees.

**Stock Purchase Agreement**
Legal document defining the terms of an equity investment.

**Strike Price**
The predetermined price at which stock options can beexercised.

**Subscription**
A business model where customers pay a recurring fee (monthly or annually) for continued access to a product or service.

**Sweat Equity**
Ownership earned through work and effort rather than financial investment.

**SWOT Analysis**
Strategic planning tool evaluating Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats.

---

## T

**TAM (Total Addressable Market)**
The total revenue opportunity available if a product achieved 100% market share.

**Target Market**
The specific group of customers a company aims to reach with its products or services.

**Term Sheet**
A non-binding agreement outlining the major terms of an investment, negotiated before final legal documents.

**Traction**
Evidence that your product is gaining market acceptance, typically measured by user growth, revenue, or engagement.

**Trademark**
A symbol, word, or phrase legally registered to represent a company or product, protecting brand identity.

**Trade Secret**
Confidential business information that provides competitive advantage, legally protected without registration.

**Transaction**
An exchange of value, typically a sale or purchase.

**Transparency**
Openly sharing information with stakeholders, including employees, investors, and sometimes customers.

---

## U

**UI (User Interface)**
The visual elements and layout through which users interact with a product.

**Unicorn**
A privately-held startup valued at over $1 billion.

**Unit Economics**
The revenues and costs associated with a single unit of product or customer, used to assess business viability.

**Upround**
A financing round at a higher valuation than the previous round. Opposite of down round.

**User Experience (UX)**
The overall experience and satisfaction a person has when using a product or service.

**User Story**
A simple description of a feature from the user's perspective. Format: "As a [user], I want [feature] so that [benefit]."

---

## V

**Valuation**
The estimated worth of a company, typically determined during funding rounds.

**Valuation Cap**
In a SAFE or convertible note, the maximum valuation at which the investment converts to equity, protecting early investors.

**Value Proposition**
A clear statement of the tangible benefits a customer receives from using your product or service.

**VC (Venture Capital)**
Money invested in startups with high growth potential, typically in exchange for equity.

**Venture Capitalist**
An investor or firm that provides venture capital to startups.

**Vesting**
The process by which an employee, founder, or advisor earns their equity over time. Protects the company if someone leaves early.

**Vesting Schedule**
The timeline over which equity vests. Standard is 4 years with a 1-year cliff.

**Viral Coefficient**
The number of new users each existing user brings. A coefficient >1 means exponential growth.

**Vision Statement**
An aspirational description of what a company aims to achieve in the future.

---

## W

**Waterfall**
A sequential project management approach where each phase must be completed before the next begins. Opposite of Agile.

**Wireframe**
A basic visual guide showing the skeletal framework of a website or app before design details are added.

**Working Capital**
The capital available for day-to-day operations. Formula: Current Assets - Current Liabilities.

---

## X

**X-Factor**
A unique, often intangible quality that makes a startup stand out and likely to succeed.

---

## Y

**YoY (Year-over-Year)**
Comparison of performance between the same period in consecutive years.

**Y Combinator (YC)**
One of the most prestigious startup accelerators, known for funding companies like Airbnb, Dropbox, and Stripe.

---

## Z

**Zombie Company**
A company that generates just enough revenue to survive but not enough to significantly grow or become profitable.

**Zero to One**
The concept (from Peter Thiel's book) of creating something entirely new rather than copying existing ideas. Innovation vs. iteration.
